بصراحة

Past simple vs. present perfect (in speech)

Use past simple for a finished action at a specific time. Use present perfect when the time isn't mentioned, or the result matters now.

✅ "I finished the report yesterday." (specific time → past simple)
✅ "I've already finished the report." (result now → present perfect)
🚫 "I have finished it yesterday." — don't mix a finished-time word with present perfect.

Future forms: will vs. going to

"Going to" = a plan you already made. "Will" = a decision made right now, or a prediction.

✅ "I'm going to visit my parents this weekend." (already planned)
✅ "I think I'll call her later." (decided in the moment)

Basic linking words for flow

Instead of stacking short sentences with "and... and... and", connect ideas with: because, so, although, but, when.

✅ "I was tired, so I went to bed early, although I still had work to finish."
Warm-up

Describe your typical school day, start to finish.

Aim for 60 seconds. Use at least 3 time-linking words: first, then, after that, finally.

Past experience

Tell me about a lesson that didn't go as planned.

Use past simple for what happened, present perfect for the lasting effect: "Since then, I've always..."

Opinion

Do you prefer teaching math or science? Why?

Give one reason, one example, one small contrast ("but on the other hand...").

Common mistakes at this level

🚫 "I am agree with you." → ✅ "I agree with you." (agree is a verb, not an adjective)
🚫 "She explained me the lesson." → ✅ "She explained the lesson to me."
🚫 "I have 25 years." → ✅ "I am 25 years old."

Filler phrases to sound more natural

Native speakers rarely speak in perfect, unbroken sentences. These small fillers buy you thinking time without long silences.

"Well, let me think..." · "Actually, you know what..." · "I mean, basically..."

Quick self-check

Record yourself doing one Speaking Drill above. Play it back and count: how many times did you pause for more than 3 seconds? That number should shrink week by week.

بشكل عام

All three conditionals in conversation

1st (real future), 2nd (unreal present), 3rd (unreal past) — natives mix these fluidly when speaking.

✅ 1st: "If I get the job, I'll move schools." (likely)
✅ 2nd: "If I were the principal, I'd change the schedule." (hypothetical)
✅ 3rd: "If I had known about the interview earlier, I would have prepared more." (past, unreal)

Passive voice for formal/neutral tone

Use passive when the action matters more than who did it — common in reports and professional speech.

✅ "The exams were graded last week." (not: "I graded the exams")
✅ "New guidelines have been introduced this term."

Register shifting mid-conversation

Same idea, different register. Recognize both and practice switching on cue.

Casual: "Can you send me that file?"
Formal: "Would you be able to share that file with me, please?"
Register switch

Explain the same classroom problem twice: once to a close colleague, once to the school principal.

Notice which words change — not the facts, just the tone and structure.

Conditional practice

If you were offered a job at an international school abroad tomorrow, what would you do?

Use 2nd conditional throughout: "If I were offered..., I would..."

Structured opinion

Should homework be given every day? Argue one side, then acknowledge the other with "whereas" or "nevertheless."

Claim → one piece of evidence → one contrasting point → conclusion, under 2 minutes.

Common mistakes at this level

🚫 "I'm agree that it's a good idea, despite of the cost." → ✅ "I agree that it's a good idea, despite the cost."
🚫 "It depends of the situation." → ✅ "It depends on the situation."
🚫 "I look forward to hear from you." → ✅ "I look forward to hearing from you." (gerund after "to" here)

Softening disagreement (a B2 register skill)

At B1 you might just say "no, that's wrong." At B2, disagreement is softened without losing the point.

"I see your point, but I'd argue that..." · "That's fair, although I think..."

Quick self-check

Take one Speaking Drill above and redo it twice: once assuming you're talking to a friend, once assuming a school inspector is listening. If both versions sound identical, that's the register gap to close next.

يتحفظ في التصريح

Inversion for emphasis (formal speech)

Flipping subject/verb order after negative or limiting expressions adds emphasis, common in advanced speech and writing.

✅ "Never have I seen such a well-organized classroom."
✅ "Not only did she pass, but she also scored the highest in the class."

Cleft sentences to control focus

Split a sentence to highlight exactly what matters — very common in nuanced spoken English.

✅ "What really surprised me was how quickly they adapted." (instead of: "They adapted quickly, which surprised me")
✅ "It's the lack of preparation that concerns me most."

Hedging language for nuance

Native speakers rarely state opinions as flat facts — they soften claims to sound careful, not weak.

✅ "It could be argued that the test was too long."
✅ "There's a case to be made for shorter school days."
Implicit meaning

Listen to a native podcast clip and explain what the speaker implied but didn't say directly.

Focus on tone, hesitation, and word choice — not just the literal words.

Unprepared opinion

Should international schools standardize curricula across countries? You have 30 seconds to prepare, then speak for 3 minutes.

Use at least one hedge, one cleft sentence, and one piece of inversion for emphasis.

Tone switching

Explain a scheduling conflict: first as a light joke to a friend, then as a serious concern to your school director.

Same facts, completely different tone — this is the C1 skill that matters most.

Common mistakes even at this level

🚫 "Despite of the criticism, the policy stayed." → ✅ "Despite the criticism, the policy stayed."
🚫 "It was such a good idea that few people expected it to fail so it did." (run-on) → ✅ Break into two clean clauses; even advanced speakers over-pack sentences under pressure.
🚫 Overusing "very" (very good, very important) → ✅ Replace with precise words: outstanding, crucial, essential.

Signaling nuance without over-explaining

At C1, you can trust the listener to infer — you don't need to spell out every implication.

"Let's just say the meeting didn't go as planned." (implies more without stating it)
"I'll leave that one there." (signals you're choosing not to elaborate, deliberately)

Quick self-check

Pick a recent conversation in Arabic where you made a subtle point through tone rather than words. Try to recreate that exact effect in English — that gap is the real C1 target, not vocabulary size.
